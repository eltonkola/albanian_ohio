📊 Executive Summary

Ohio is home to one of the most significant Albanian and Kosovar communities in the United States, anchored by two major clusters: Greater Cleveland (the older, larger hub centered on Lakewood, Fairview Park, and Rocky River) and Columbus / Central Ohio (a growing community with strong post-1990s Kosovo-origin roots).

Official ACS ancestry data records approximately 4,100 people reporting Albanian ancestry statewide. Cross-referencing with birthplace data (Albania and Kosovo-born), the PUMS broad estimate (adding Albania/Kosovo-born who didn't report Albanian ancestry) rises to ~4,600. Community estimates place the true number significantly higher — see the Data Sources section for why Census data undercounts this population.

Why Census Data Undercounts Albanians in Ohio
The ACS ancestry question captures only those who write "Albanian." Many ethnic Albanians from Kosovo report "Kosovar," "Yugoslav," or "Serbian." Those from North Macedonia often report "Macedonian." Those from Montenegro may report "Montenegrin." Second/third generation individuals may report mixed ancestry. Census undercounting of refugee and immigrant communities is well-documented. Cross-referencing ancestry, birthplace, and language data yields significantly higher estimates.

📚 Understanding the Data Sources

What is the ACS (American Community Survey)?

The American Community Survey is an ongoing survey conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau that collects detailed demographic, social, economic, and housing information from approximately 3.5 million households each year. Unlike the decennial Census (which counts everyone every 10 years), the ACS provides yearly estimates. The 5-Year ACS combines five years of data to produce reliable estimates for small populations and geographies — this is what we use, since Ohio's Albanian population is relatively small in statistical terms.

The ACS produces published summary tables (like B04006 for ancestry, B05006 for place of birth, B16001 for language). These are aggregated, ready-to-use counts. However, they don't let you cross-reference variables — you can't, for example, ask "how many Kosovo-born people also report Albanian ancestry?" For that, you need PUMS.

What is PUMS (Public Use Microdata Sample)?

PUMS stands for Public Use Microdata Sample. It's the individual-level (person-by-person) data behind the ACS. Each row represents one real person, with all their survey responses: ancestry, place of birth, language, education, occupation, income, age, and more. To protect privacy, PUMS strips out names and exact addresses — the smallest geography it provides is the PUMA (Public Use Microdata Area), a region of roughly 100,000–200,000 people.

Crucially, PUMS is a sample, not a full count. Each person record carries a person weight (PWGTP) indicating how many people in the real population that record represents. All population estimates in this report are weighted — we sum the weights, not the raw record counts. The Ohio 5-Year PUMS file contains ~584,000 records representing Ohio's ~11.8 million residents.

Key ACS Tables We Use

TableNameWhat It Tells Us
B04006 People Reporting Ancestry How many people wrote "Albanian" as their ancestry. This is the most-cited number but dramatically undercounts the community because Kosovars, Macedonian Albanians, and others often report different ancestry labels.
B05006 Place of Birth (Foreign-Born) How many people were born in Albania, Kosovo, North Macedonia, Montenegro, etc. Captures foreign-born regardless of what ancestry they reported.
B16001 Language Spoken at Home How many people speak Albanian at home. Useful as a supplementary indicator, though many second-generation Albanians speak only English.

Why “Broad Estimate” ≠ “ACS Ancestry Count”
The ACS ancestry count (~4,100 for Ohio) only includes people who explicitly wrote "Albanian" as their ancestry. The PUMS broad estimate (~4,600) adds people born in Albania or Kosovo who didn't report Albanian ancestry. Both numbers are likely significant undercounts — community organizations estimate the true ethnic Albanian population in Ohio at 10,000–25,000, since many Kosovars report "Kosovar" or "European" ancestry, and second-generation Americans may report no specific ancestry at all.

🎓 Education & Language

Education-Employment Paradox (Harvard Growth Lab Finding)

The Harvard CID/Growth Lab study (Nedelkoska & Khaw, 2015) found a striking education-employment mismatch among first-generation Albanian Americans: they are more educated than comparable non-Albanian Americans, yet over-represented in low-skill occupations and earn less than comparable peers. This reflects credential recognition barriers, language challenges, and occupational channeling into ethnic-network industries (construction, food service).

The second generation shows a reversal: low-skilled Albanian Americans earn significantly more than peers (ethnic network premium), while highly-skilled earn less (possible barriers in professional advancement).

Source: Nedelkoska & Khaw (2015). "The Albanian Community in the United States." Harvard CID Working Paper.

💼 Work & Income

✈️ Migration Timeline

The year-of-entry distribution reveals the key migration waves: a small pre-1980 cohort from early/mid-20th century immigration, a moderate 1980s flow, the massive post-Communist wave of the 1990s (driven by Albania's collapse in 1991 and the Kosovo war in 1998-99), continued family reunification in the 2000s, and a tapering flow in recent years.

📖 Historical Context

1890s-1900s — First Wave
Early Albanian immigrants arrive in Ohio's industrial cities. Cleveland becomes a primary destination, drawn by factory and steel mill jobs. Most from Korçë region in southern Albania.
1900-1938 — Community Formation
Sustained immigration. Albanian newspapers, Orthodox and Muslim institutions established in Cleveland. Community infrastructure takes shape.
1945-1960 — Post-WWII Displaced Persons
Refugees from DP camps in Italy, Germany, Austria. Ethnic Albanians from Kosovo and Macedonia displaced by WWII and early Communist Yugoslavia. Many settle in Cleveland's west side.
1961-1990 — Cold War Era
Albania seals borders under Hoxha's Communist regime. Limited immigration from Kosovo/Macedonia within Yugoslavia. Cleveland community grows via natural increase.
1991-1997 — Post-Communist Wave
Fall of Albanian communism (1991) triggers massive emigration. Thousands arrive in Ohio, settling in Cleveland and Columbus. Columbus Albanian community begins significant growth.
1998-1999 — Kosovo War Major Wave
Kosovo War displaces 1+ million ethnic Albanians. U.S. admits ~13,000 refugees. Columbus, a major refugee resettlement city via CRIS Ohio, receives a significant number. This wave transforms Columbus into a major Albanian hub.
2000-Present — Continued Growth
Family reunification, diversity lottery, and continued flows from Kosovo, Albania, North Macedonia, and Montenegro. Columbus community continues to grow and formalize.

Reproducibility — How to Verify & Improve

This project is designed to be fully reproducible. Here's how:

  1. Clone the repo and install Python dependencies: pip install -r scripts/requirements.txt
  2. Get a Census API key (free): api.census.gov/data/key_signup.html
  3. Download raw Census tables: python scripts/01_download_census_data.py
  4. Download Ohio PUMS from Census FTP → unzip to data/raw/
  5. Process PUMS: python scripts/02_process_pums.py
  6. Open index.html — all charts auto-load from the JSON in data/processed/

Contributions welcome! Add new data sources, fix POBP codes, improve PUMA mappings, or enhance the visualizations.
